In an unusually early 'girl group battle,' major K-pop girl groups from all four of Korea's top entertainment companies made comebacks in May 2026. BABYMONSTER (YG Entertainment) kicked things off on May 4 with 'CHOOM,' followed by NMIXX (JYP Entertainment) on May 11, ITZY (JYP Entertainment) on May 18, LE SSERAFIM (Source Music under HYBE) on May 22, and aespa (SM Entertainment) on May 29.

As a result, major music streaming charts have been filled with new girl group tracks, including aespa's 'WDA' and 'LEMONADE,' NMIXX's 'Heavy Serenade,' and LE SSERAFIM's 'BOOMPALA.' This stands in contrast to the usual pattern of girl groups releasing music in the summer, when upbeat dance tracks tend to perform well and various events are plentiful.

Industry insiders point to the globalization of world tours as a key reason for the May timing. An official from a major entertainment company stated, 'An album absolutely has to come out by May if we want to complete a proper world tour after promotions.' The same official added, 'As K-pop has grown in popularity, it's become difficult to secure performance venues not just in Korea but also in Japan, Southeast Asia, and the Americas. The earlier the album drops, the better, given how long tours can stretch due to venue availability issues.'

An official from another major label echoed this view: 'Venues become increasingly hard to book as the year goes on. Considering all the practical constraints, starting a tour in the summer is ideal, and May was each group's calculated comeback timing with that in mind.' Indeed, BABYMONSTER will launch their new world tour in June at Seoul's Jamsil Indoor Stadium, LE SSERAFIM in July at Inspire Arena in Incheon, and aespa in August at Seoul's Gocheok Sky Dome.

The so-called 'chart reverse-run marketing' trend has also played a role. Unlike male artists such as BTS or G-Dragon, who shoot straight to the top of charts on the strength of massive fandoms, girl groups that rely more on general public appeal — like ILLIT's 'It's Me' — tend to climb the charts gradually over a longer period. An official from a prominent agency noted, 'These days it's common for a song to hit No. 1 on streaming charts long after release. Putting a song out now means the response will be hottest during the summer peak season.'

The upcoming 2026 FIFA World Cup, opening June 11 in North and Central America, is also cited as a factor. An industry source explained that during the World Cup, music shows are frequently cancelled, drastically reducing performance opportunities for artists, and that all forms of promotion — online viral campaigns, news coverage, YouTube — become difficult. Data journalist Kim Jin-woo of Circle Chart noted that music streaming usage dropped month-on-month during the 2010, 2014, and 2018 World Cups. He analyzed: 'Girl group comebacks usually concentrate in June and July, but this year they clustered in May. When a major sports event is on, media attention is divided, so it appears the industry adjusted comeback schedules accordingly.' Smaller and mid-sized agency acts such as Billlie, Queenz Eye, and XLOVE also returned with new albums this month, similarly looking to avoid the World Cup period.
