While concert tours across the United States are being scaled back or cancelled due to high inflation, BTS is drawing widespread attention for selling out every date on their North American and European ARIRANG world tour, which launched in April 2026.

According to the music industry on June 19, Forbes magazine last month published an article titled 'Blue Dot Fever Is Hitting Musicians,' highlighting the downturn in the US live entertainment market. 'Blue Dot Fever' is a newly coined term referring to the spread of unsold seats — displayed as blue dots on major US ticketing platform Ticketmaster — at concerts that would have sold out in the past.

Forbes reported that the Pussycat Dolls significantly scaled back their US arena and stadium tour due to poor ticket sales, while Post Malone and Jelly Roll cut one-third of their joint US stadium tour dates. Meghan Trainor and Jain cancelled their entire US arena tour schedules. Forbes cited surging inflation as the primary cause, noting that the average concert ticket price this year is $144, up sharply from $82 in 2020 and $115 last year. The outlet also pointed to skyrocketing gas prices following the Iran war lowering profit margins for touring, and the FIFA World Cup in North and Central America this summer as a major competing entertainment draw.

Against this backdrop, BTS's new stadium world tour ARIRANG has captured industry attention. The ARIRANG tour is BTS's first concert tour since 'Permission To Dance On Stage' in 2022, and their first full world tour covering Europe and Asia since 'LOVE YOURSELF: SPEAK YOURSELF' in 2019 — a seven-year gap. All North American and European dates sold out, and the tour expanded from an originally announced 79 shows to 88, with one show added each in Tampa, Stanford, and Las Vegas in the US, Lima in Peru, Santiago in Chile, Buenos Aires in Argentina, Melbourne in Australia, Jakarta in Indonesia, and Bulacan in the Philippines.

Billboard reported that BTS topped the monthly Top Tours chart after just eight shows held in April across Goyang (South Korea), Tokyo (Japan), and Tampa (US), generating $76.2 million in revenue and selling 417,000 tickets. The three Tampa shows alone recorded the highest revenue and largest attendance of any single venue worldwide in April. Industry insiders attribute the tour's success to the strong unity of ARMY (the fandom name) after enduring a four-year hiatus during the members' mandatory military service, as well as the wide range of offline events connected to the concerts.

BTS's large-scale offline fan event 'THE CITY,' held in cities including Seoul, Busan, and Las Vegas, transformed concerts into comprehensive tourism experiences. A spokesperson for the Las Vegas Convention and Visitors Authority stated, 'Excluding mega sporting events like the Super Bowl or Formula One, BTS is the first time in Las Vegas history that an entire city has mobilized for a single artist,' calling it 'an unprecedented project where the entire city operated as one cultural festival.' An entertainment industry official added that 'BTS concerts have evolved into a global cultural-economic platform that moves people, stimulates local economies, and connects industries — even in a difficult market environment.'
