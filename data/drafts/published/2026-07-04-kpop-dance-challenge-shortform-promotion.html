Dance challenges optimized for short-form platforms have become one of the most important promotional tools in the K-pop market, with everyone from rookie groups to global superstar BTS jumping in. Cases of songs that failed to make a splash on music charts right after release but later climbed to the top after gaining traction through challenges are occurring one after another.

As buzz generated on TikTok and Instagram — the main channels for challenges — translates into real revenue, music labels are increasingly factoring challenges into the planning stage, crafting choruses and choreography with short-form content in mind. Girl group ILLIT's 'It's Me' debuted at No. 131 on Melon's daily chart right after release, but after a series of challenge videos featuring veteran singer Lee Jung-hyun went viral, the song climbed as high as No. 2. Tame Impala's 'Dracula', a collaboration with BLACKPINK's Jennie, spread rapidly on TikTok and other short-form platforms following the release of a remix version featuring Jennie in February, eventually charting at No. 10 on the Billboard Hot 100.

Rookie group CORTIS saw their song 'REDRED' become one of the biggest hits of the year, reaching No. 1 on Melon's Top 100 — an unusual feat for a boy group — after a challenge featuring a powerful chorus and striking choreography gained widespread attention. TOURS' 'OVERDRIVE' and Choi Ye-na's 'CatchCatch' also benefited significantly from challenge-driven promotion. One music label official said, 'There are various promotions before an idol comeback, but challenges have become the promotional method best suited to the short-form era,' adding, 'It's no longer optional — it's essential.'

As challenges have emerged as a key variable in a song's success, more labels are factoring short-form platforms into the planning stage from the start — crafting addictive choruses and designing separate 'point choreography' of around 15 seconds. Many idols also prepare an easier version of the choreography specifically for challenges, and some labels release a challenge-specific audio track ahead of a song's official release date. BOYNEXTDOOR, who released their new song 'VIRAL' last month, said in a recent interview, 'A lot of people enjoy challenges and Shorts, so we're thinking about how best to use them,' and added, 'We don't know which of the various things will take off, but we hope at least one goes viral.'

BTS leader RM spoke about challenges on the fan platform Weverse Live, saying, 'With the Hooligan challenge, fan-made choreography branched off into a new challenge, which helped it spread even further.' He added, 'A challenge isn't something you can force to happen. I was glad that so many people did the Hooligan challenge — thanks to the fan-made version.' According to music and entertainment data outlet Luminate, 84% of songs that entered the Billboard Global 200 chart in 2024 had already gone viral on TikTok before charting. Additionally, 38% of Gen Z users who consume music content on Instagram daily were classified as 'super fans' who actively attend concerts and buy merchandise — with concert attendance rates 45% higher than general users and physical album purchase rates at 21%, roughly double the 12% of general users.

However, criticism has also emerged that challenges, now a standard part of every comeback, are pushing the industry to focus more on generating buzz than on creating good music. It has become routine for K-pop idol groups to film challenge videos with other artists during waiting time at TV music show recordings, consuming considerable time and energy. Some idols even take separate dance lessons specifically for challenges, and which high-profile idol group a label can secure for a challenge is considered an important management capability. Pop music critic Jung Min-jae noted, 'Challenges are a prime example of how music is consumed in a short-form-centered era and have become a basic promotional tool. However, as K-pop shows a tendency to focus only on short-form choruses and point choreography rather than the song as a whole, there needs to be reflection on over-relying on buzz at the expense of the music itself.'
