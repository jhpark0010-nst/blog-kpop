BTS made history on July 1, 2026, with their first-ever concert in Belgium, drawing approximately 65,000 fans to King Baudouin Stadium in Brussels. ARMY members traveled from across Europe — including the UK, the Netherlands, Italy, and Sweden — as well as from the United States, making the event a truly global gathering.

Getting to the stadium was no easy feat. Brussels public transport operator STIB doubled the frequency of Line 6 subway trains running to Baudouin Stadium, yet the carriages remained packed to capacity. A STIB staff member managing the crowds told a puzzled elderly passenger, 'We doubled the subway service because of BTS, and it still looks like this.' Despite the heat and the crush — with no air conditioning in the packed cars — fans arrived with visible excitement on their faces.

On the subway, a couple in their twenties — April, originally from the Philippines, and her boyfriend Alex — said they had come all the way from Manchester, UK. They had originally tried to book tickets for the London shows on July 6 and 7 but failed, and chose Brussels as their alternative. An 18-year-old Finnish high school student named Ronia said she had grown up listening to BTS since primary school and had spent three years saving her allowance to finally see them live.

Outside the stadium, fans waited in a queue that moved slowly due to only one exit gate being opened for safety reasons — a wait of roughly 20 minutes just to get above ground. Rather than complain, the crowd sang BTS songs together, including 'Arirang.' A 26-year-old Belgian fan named Benoit arrived dressed in a traditional Korean gat hat and dopo robe, saying he had fallen in love with hanbok during a visit to a royal palace in Korea the previous year. He had purchased the dopo in Paris and ordered the gat directly from Korea.

Veronica (53), an Italian fan from Florence who had hand-lettered 'Brussels' in Korean on her T-shirt, arrived four days early with her 25-year-old daughter Sharon as her 'bodyguard.' She said it was her first time ever visiting Brussels, all thanks to BTS, and that BTS was playing a role in connecting Europeans together. Nearby, a Brazilian-Swedish man named Rodrik was handing out 40,000 self-printed flyers reading 'ARIRANG — Forever Together, BTS,' asking concertgoers to hold them up when the crowd sang 'Arirang' during the show. He said he became a fan through his Korean-born wife, who was adopted in Sweden as a baby, adding with a laugh that hearing 'Arirang' made him feel he could understand the emotions Koreans feel.

A police officer named Kenny, in his thirties, noted that the evening also had a Belgium vs. Senegal World Cup round-of-32 match scheduled, making it a busy night — but said he had no major concerns about the BTS concert. 'Look at the crowd coming out of the subway station. Isn't it over 95% women?' he said. A fellow officer named Kena added that while he himself was not a particular fan, his wife and young son were devoted ARMY members, and that anyone who enjoys BTS's wholesome, lyrical music was nothing to worry about from a public safety standpoint. Outside the venue, a local Korean restaurant selling kimbap, tteokbokki, and yangnyeom chicken drew a steady stream of customers, showcasing the growing popularity of Korean street food. The Brussels concerts continue through July 2.
