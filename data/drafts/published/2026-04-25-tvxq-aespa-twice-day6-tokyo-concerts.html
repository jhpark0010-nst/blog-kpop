TVXQ, TWICE, aespa, and DAY6 are set to draw more than 400,000 fans to Tokyo this weekend and into early next week, as top K-pop acts from the 2nd through 4th generations hold simultaneous concerts at major venues across the Japanese capital.

TVXQ, a 2nd-generation representative boy group, will hold a solo concert at Nissan Stadium — one of Japan's largest venues, with a capacity of up to 75,000 per show — on April 25 and 26. TVXQ became the first overseas act to perform at Nissan Stadium in 2013, and in 2018 performed there for three consecutive days, a record for any artist, domestic or foreign.

This marks TVXQ's third time performing at Nissan Stadium, adding a record for the most solo concerts there by an overseas act. Meanwhile, 3rd-generation girl group TWICE will perform at Tokyo's National Olympic Stadium — which holds up to 80,000 per show — on April 25, 26, and 28, becoming the first overseas act to hold a solo concert at that venue. TWICE will perform on a 360-degree open stage for a fully immersive experience.

TWICE previously became the first K-pop girl group to perform three consecutive days at Tokyo Dome in 2022, and in 2024 became the first K-pop girl group to perform at Nissan Stadium, following stadium shows in Osaka, Nagoya, and Fukuoka in 2023.

4th-generation girl group aespa will hold a solo concert at Tokyo Dome — widely regarded as a dream venue in Japan — on April 25 and 26. DAY6, the group credited with sparking a band boom in the K-pop market, will also meet fans at Tokyo Keio Arena during the same period.

Combined attendance across all four acts is estimated to exceed at least 400,000, even accounting for reduced seating due to stage setups — a crowd comparable to the population of a major regional city in South Korea. Professor Kim Jeong-seop of Sungshin Women's University commented: 'Japan remains a very important market for K-pop.' He added: 'Japanese fans have a devoted, wholehearted quality — once they fall for something, they enjoy it for a long time. Even during sensitive periods in the bilateral relationship, they characteristically separate politics from culture and continue consuming K-pop.'
