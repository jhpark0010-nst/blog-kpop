Major K-pop agencies are increasingly offering Korean fans priority ticket access for domestic concerts. JYP Entertainment recently announced that domestic fan club members will be able to book tickets first for Stray Kids' new world tour, RUN IT, which kicks off in Seoul on July 25. A portion of all seats will be made available to Korean fans on June 29 at 8 p.m., with global ticketing opening the following day, June 30, at 8 p.m.

The system is structured so that tickets can only be purchased from within the region where a fan club account was pre-verified, meaning overseas residents cannot participate in the domestic presale. Online, Korean fans responded positively, with comments such as 'It's great to see a domestic presale introduced' and 'I hope this system continues.'

HYBE labels had already adopted a similar approach, with CORTIS under Big Hit Music and ENHYPEN under Belift Lab both implementing domestic presales. SM Entertainment followed suit — after Red Velvet, NCT DREAM offered Korean fans priority ticketing on June 23, and aespa did the same on June 24.

The primary reason major K-pop agencies have introduced this system is to protect the concert-going opportunities of domestic fans. As K-pop has gone global, large numbers of overseas fans have flocked to idol concerts held in Korea, leading to side effects such as scalping and reselling driven by buyers abroad. In particular, since the so-called hallyu restriction policy in China made it difficult to hold K-pop concerts there, the influx of Chinese-speaking fans into Korean concerts surged sharply, intensifying what fans call 'fierce ticketing' and generating sustained complaints from domestic fans.

The music industry expects the domestic fan club presale system to ease some of these frustrations. One official at a major agency said, 'It is a method that can further increase the opportunities and possibilities for Korean audiences to attend domestic concerts, which is why positive reactions continue among local fans.' Another official said, 'This is a method that should have been introduced much earlier. Given that K-pop artists visit many countries and regions on tour, there does not appear to be a major issue in terms of fairness. It will be an opportunity for artists and fandoms to connect more deeply in Korea, where K-pop originated.'

However, voices of concern have also emerged over the potential sense of exclusion felt by global fans, given how significant overseas fanbases are to the K-pop market. There are worries that the policy could generate dissatisfaction among international fans who hold considerable purchasing power. One official at an idol agency noted, 'The intent is to look after Korean fans in recognition of the symbolic importance of the Seoul concert as the starting point of a global tour,' but added, 'From an agency's perspective, we have no choice but to think deeply about the sentiments of overseas fans, who are a core customer base, so finding the right balance is important.'
