Fueled by BTS and the broader K-pop wave, Korean has emerged as one of the top three languages in the world's largest music market. According to music and entertainment data firm Luminate, Korean accounted for a 1.1% share of the US music streaming market in Q1 2026, ranking third behind English (86.0%) and Spanish (9.5%).

Korean's share has held steady in recent years: 0.7% in 2023, 0.7% in 2024, 1.1% in 2025, and 1.1% again in Q1 2026. Meanwhile, Spanish has seen notable growth, rising from 8.1% in 2023 to 9.4% in 2024, dipping to 8.9% in 2025, and climbing back to 9.5% in Q1 2026 — approaching the 10% mark — driven in part by Latin superstar Bad Bunny. Luminate is a well-established data provider that supplies Billboard chart data, among other services.

Regarding Korean, Luminate stated: 'Korean-language streaming firmly maintained a 1.1% share in Q1 2026, powered by K-pop sensations including BTS.' The firm also noted that languages other than English, Spanish, and Korean combined for 3.4%, an increase of 1.5 percentage points compared to the previous year.

Luminate added: 'With Bad Bunny riding the momentum from his historic Super Bowl halftime show appearance and BTS returning as a full group, even the average American music listener will clearly feel that a cultural shift is taking place.' It further analyzed: 'The music industry is more globalized than ever, and international artists can now achieve the kind of global recognition that was once only possible for English-language acts.'

Korea's presence was also evident in the breakdown by country of origin in the US streaming market. Excluding the United States itself, Korea ranked 5th with a 1.8% share, behind the UK (7.8%), Mexico (5.0%), Canada (4.5%), and Puerto Rico (3.1%) — surpassing Australia (1.5%).

Luminate observed: 'In the past, international artists seeking commercial success in the US had to incorporate Western styles or sing in English. But today, streaming infrastructure allows fans to discover music organically, and language is no longer a barrier to entry — it has become a medium of connection.' It added that 'to stay competitive, labels will need to scout international artists, pursue cross-border collaborations, and develop multilingual marketing strategies to match shifting consumer tastes.'
