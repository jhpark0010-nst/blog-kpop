A new report has found that BTS's Netflix comeback live generated mass real-time gathering, while the accompanying documentary sparked in-depth interpretation and debate. Korea Broadcasting Advertising Corporation (KOBACO) published the report on May 19, comparing online reaction patterns for two Netflix titles — 'BTS Comeback Live: Arirang' and the documentary 'BTS: The Return' — using data from its broadcast content value analysis system, RACOI.

The report was written by Professor Kwon Bo-ram of Catholic University and covers four weeks of online reactions to both titles, which were released in March.

According to the analysis, 'Arirang,' as a live broadcast, generated an explosive level of buzz in its opening week: 3,212 posts, 2.93 million video views, and 1,021 news articles.

By contrast, 'The Return' recorded a relatively modest 297 posts, but the average number of comments per post was 11.3 — more than four times the 2.6 average for 'Arirang' — indicating that active exchanges of opinion took place among users.

KOBACO noted that although both titles were released on the same platform at the same time, they moved online users in different ways. Reactions to 'Arirang' formed around keywords related to physical attendance and movement — such as 'Gwanghwamun,' 'performance,' 'ticketing,' and 'concert' — even before its release. After release, keywords like 'live broadcast,' 'live,' 'traffic,' and 'control' spread widely. KOBACO characterized this as a pattern of event-type content consumption that mobilized both urban space and online platforms simultaneously. Two weeks after release, expressions such as 'Billboard,' 'moving,' 'cheering,' and 'thank you' became central, shifting toward language of performance achievement and retrospection.

For 'The Return,' keywords such as 'lyrics,' 'opinion,' 'meaning,' and 'work' stood out after release, sustaining a flow of content explanation and interpretation. Comments spiked immediately after release and discourse around the work's artistic merit and narrative continued thereafter, KOBACO explained. The report concluded that 'K-pop comebacks are no longer simply album releases, but are evolving into complex media events combining urban space, live streaming, documentary narrative, and news coverage.' It further noted that 'strategy is increasingly important not only in terms of how much buzz to generate, but also in designing what meaning and discourse the content will leave behind after release.'
