South Korea's Fair Trade Commission (FTC) has identified unfair terms and conditions in paid fan club memberships operated by 24 companies — including 18 entertainment agencies and 6 fandom platform companies — and ordered them to make voluntary corrections. The memberships, which offer benefits such as priority concert ticketing and exclusive merchandise purchases, are priced in the range of 10,000 to 50,000 Korean won.

Refund restriction clauses were among the most prominent issues flagged. Big Hit Music had specified that refunds are not available after 7 days from sign-up or once any membership benefit has been used. Starship Entertainment similarly stated that cancellations are possible within 7 days of payment, but refunds are unavailable if any benefit has been received even in part. The FTC determined that restricting mid-term cancellations and refunds when members are dissatisfied with the benefits constitutes an unfairly disadvantageous clause for consumers.

Under the corrected terms, companies agreed to offer full refunds within 7 days of sign-up if no benefits have been used. After 7 days or if benefits have been used, a refund of the remaining balance will be issued after deducting a penalty fee — typically 10% of the membership fee — and the value of benefits already used.

The FTC also flagged clauses that unfairly exempt companies from obligations and liability. SM Entertainment had stated that if a membership is renewed and then cancelled, the remaining validity period from the previous membership would not be restored. The FTC noted that companies are in fact obligated to restore consumers to the state with remaining validity when a renewal is cancelled. YG Entertainment had stipulated that no refund would be issued in cases where content related to a changed member cannot be provided 'without fault on the part of YG Entertainment' due to reasons such as the addition, withdrawal, or replacement of individual members in an artist group.

Kakao Entertainment had stated it would not be responsible for service disruptions caused by the member's own fault, and CJ E&M included a clause disclaiming liability for damages arising from illegal third-party access to servers or illegal use of servers. The FTC stated that these clauses 'uniformly exempt the operator's liability for service provision or damages even in cases that could be attributed to the operator's management sphere, such as an artist's withdrawal,' and ordered corrections.

Antenna and Weverse Company had also included language allowing them to suspend membership services 'for business reasons,' which the FTC said was too vague. Blue Garage, which operates JYP Entertainment's paid membership, had allowed contract termination based on ambiguous grounds such as 'reasonable judgment,' while Nomuss had specified that termination was possible without prior notice. Going forward, companies agreed to define specific and clear grounds for contract termination and usage restrictions, and to give consumers an opportunity to respond before any action is taken. Additional clauses flagged as unfair included unilateral post deletion and overly broad provisions on third-party sharing and retention of personal data.

Kwak Go-eun, head of the FTC's Terms and Special Transactions Division, said, 'As the K-pop market continues to expand globally, we proactively reviewed unfair terms in fan club paid membership services,' adding that the action 'will serve as an important opportunity to protect consumer rights within the industry and establish a fair and transparent trading order befitting the scale of the K-pop fandom.' Companies plan to correct refund-related clauses within the year, with other clauses to be revised as soon as possible.
