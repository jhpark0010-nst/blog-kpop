TVXQ, TWICE, aespa, and DAY6 simultaneously held large-scale concerts at major Tokyo venues last weekend, drawing a combined total of well over 400,000 attendees. It has been 25 years since singer BoA debuted in Japan in 2001 and topped the Oricon chart the following year, yet the Korean Wave continues to burn hot in the world's second-largest music market.

Sato Mayumi, who attended the TVXQ concert at Nissan Stadium — Japan's largest concert venue — with her daughter, was asked about the secret behind the group's longevity. 'You can see that the artists sincerely respond to fans in their everyday lives,' she said. 'That makes fans want to keep supporting them for a long time. I came here today because I'm grateful for the heart that says fans are another member of the group.'

TVXQ held their third solo concert at Nissan Stadium — a venue even Japanese artists rarely fill — following previous runs in 2013 and 2018, drawing 130,000 fans over two days. TWICE performed across three shows at the National Stadium, which opened in 2019 and can hold up to 80,000 per show, using a 360-degree open stage. aespa performed at Tokyo Dome, one of Japan's premier large venues, and DAY6 held two days of concerts at Keio Arena, which holds up to 10,000. A week earlier, on April 17–18, BTS's world tour had filled Tokyo Dome, where fans sang along to the Korean folk song 'Arirang' together.

Kim Dong-woo, CEO of SM Entertainment Japan, explained the market's unique character: 'Unlike Korea, where trend cycles are fast, Japan has a culture where daughters become fans after their mothers — people love an artist for life once they fall for them.' He added, 'About 20 years ago, liking K-pop felt like consuming a niche culture, but that is absolutely not the case anymore.' Japan has never lost its title as the top importer of K-pop albums, and its fans are known for high loyalty and spending power across albums, concerts, and merchandise.

The K-pop wave in Japan has flowed from BoA's debut through second-generation acts like TVXQ, Girls' Generation, KARA, and BIGBANG, into third-generation acts like TWICE and BTS, and on to fourth-generation groups including IVE, aespa, NewJeans, and LE SSERAFIM. According to a 2023 report by music and entertainment data outlet Luminate, 39% of Japanese Gen Z women listen to K-pop. The same report found that Japan ranked first globally in streaming the songs of the top 100 K-pop acts, ahead of the United States (2nd), Indonesia (3rd), and South Korea (4th).

However, shifts are also underway. Korea's customs data shows that album exports to Japan fell 10.2% last year to approximately $80.6 million, declining for a second consecutive year. Experts note that the Japanese market is moving from physical albums — where K-pop has traditionally been strong — toward streaming, and that local Japanese idol groups have grown by borrowing elements of 'K-pop DNA,' blurring the lines. One Japanese music label official noted that while acts like KARA and Girls' Generation once dominated the market overwhelmingly, 'now it feels more like sharing the pie with local artists.' Still, industry insiders emphasize that Japanese fandom remains solid. 'Demand for K-pop in the Japanese pop music scene has grown, not shrunk,' one Japan label official said, pointing to rising streaming numbers, larger concert scales, and growing interest in diverse Korean artists beyond idol groups — including acts like Balming Tiger and Silica Gel.
