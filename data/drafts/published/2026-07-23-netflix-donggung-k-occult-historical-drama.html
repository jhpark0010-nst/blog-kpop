Netflix series 'Donggung,' released on July 17, 2026, climbed to No. 2 in the non-English shows category on the Netflix Top 10 within just three days of its premiere, drawing attention from viewers both in Korea and abroad. The show stars Nam Joo-hyuk and is set in the royal palaces of the Joseon dynasty, marking Netflix's latest attempt to capture global audiences with a Korean-language fantasy historical drama.

'Donggung' follows Gucheon (Nam Joo-hyuk), a man who can see ghosts, and Princess Saenggang (Noh Yoon-seo), a royal who can hear the voices of spirits. Acting on the orders of a king full of secrets (Cho Seung-woo), the two investigate the hidden truth behind the successive deaths of crown princes. Together, they uncover the identity of a 'pond ghost' that has been killing crown princes for 30 years, along with shocking secrets of the royal family.

The series immediately draws viewers in with its distinctive world-building, in which the story moves between the real world and the 'world of ghosts (鬼).' The two realms are visually separated through sharp color contrast — the real world rendered in cold, static blue tones, while the ghost world is depicted in deep crimson. The parallel-world structure is reminiscent of the 'Upside Down' from the Netflix hit 'Stranger Things,' and the setup in which Gucheon enters the supernatural realm through near-death experiences recalls the 2005 Hollywood film 'Constantine.'

At the same time, 'Donggung' refines distinctly Korean occult elements into its own creative identity. Writers Kwon So-ra and Seo Jae-won, who previously built Korean-style occult universes in dramas such as 'The Guest' and 'Bulgasal,' and director Choi Jeong-gyu, known for historical dramas including 'Horse Doctor' and 'Okjunghwa,' showcase the beauty of Korea through the grandeur of Joseon palaces, elegant hanbok, and flowing hangul calligraphy. Indigenous shamanic elements, led by the figure of the mudang (shaman), complete an atmosphere unique to K-occult historical drama. Supernatural beings such as won-gwi (ghosts carrying deep resentment) and gwi-mae (entities born from accumulated negative energy) were also visualized with reference to traditional Korean folklore.

A standout character is 'Kkeomeoksari,' a gwi-mae that follows Gucheon around. With large black eyes and a round clay-doll-like appearance, it has become popular online under nicknames such as 'Joseon Groot' and 'companion gwi-mae.' Viewers have compared it to Groot from Marvel's 'Guardians of the Galaxy' and Duffy from 'K-Pop Demon Hunters,' and are calling for official merchandise. Director Choi said, 'I believed the concept of a supernatural world beyond reality has existed as long as humanity itself,' adding, 'I tried to infuse a uniquely Korean flavor based on traditional Korean folklore and modern ghost stories.'

Drama critic Gong Hee-jeong noted that 'historical dramas with occult or fantasy elements have the advantage of allowing people from other cultures to immerse themselves in the story without deep historical knowledge,' and that the show 'stimulates global viewers' curiosity by dressing universal subject matter like ghosts in the new clothing of Korean traditional folklore.' However, some viewers found the narrative density and coherence lacking in the latter half, citing twists that piled up without sufficient explanation. Opinions on the acting were also divided: veteran actors Cho Seung-woo and Jang Young-nam were praised for their commanding presence, while some felt a slight disconnect in the period-specific tone and nuanced emotional performances from certain cast members. Despite these critiques, 'Donggung' has succeeded in capturing the attention of domestic and international viewers alike through its Korean-aesthetic mise-en-scène, traditional Korean music-based score, and genre-blending of K-historical drama with occult elements.
