On April 23 (local time), dozens of students at San Diego State University in California gathered on campus with focused expressions, following the choreography of BTS's 'SWIM.' It was a dance challenge held as part of a K-pop dance theory course offered as a general education elective.

The class was first established in 2023 as the first regular credited K-pop dance course at an American university, and it is led by Professor Oh Juyeon of the university's dance theory department. In a phone interview with Yonhap News on April 24, Oh said, 'Since the first class launched, student numbers have kept growing. This semester, all 80 spots filled up and the waiting list was very long.'

Professor Oh, regarded as a pioneer in K-pop dance studies abroad, trained in Korean arts middle and high schools before majoring in contemporary dance at Ewha Womans University. After completing her graduate studies at Ewha, she began her doctoral program at the University of Texas at Austin in 2010, where she developed a serious interest in K-pop dance. Though she had devoted herself entirely to concert dance — from Korean traditional dance to ballet and contemporary — her focus shifted toward dance as popular culture after entering graduate school. As someone versed in many dance genres, she found K-pop dance compelling because it blended all of them.

'When I first went to the United States, K-pop's status was nothing like it is now,' she said. 'Even in 2013, BTS was reportedly handing out flyers on American streets, so most people had no idea what K-pop was. I watched a lot of Korean artists on YouTube out of homesickness, and around that time I got the sense that K-pop's center of gravity was shifting heavily toward dance. I had an intuitive conviction that K-pop would eventually become an independent genre, like hip-hop.'

Oh went on to write her doctoral dissertation on K-pop dance when almost no English-language references existed, and she succeeded in persuading conservative faculty — who considered only theater-based dance to be art — to establish the K-pop dance course. In the meantime, K-pop's profile rose noticeably: the English edition of her book K-pop Dance became a category bestseller on Amazon, and her class remained constantly full due to its popularity among students from other departments. 'A significant number of students grew up listening to K-pop from a young age,' she said. 'They had a lot of information about artists and songs but lacked background knowledge about Korean culture or the K-pop industry. When they encounter academic explanations of something they love, their eyes light up.'

Oh recently released a Korean-language edition of K-pop Dance — originally published in the United States in 2023 — which she translated herself and updated for Korean readers. The book covers the basics of K-pop dance and analyzes the characteristics of K-pop fandoms formed through social media. 'K-pop fans dress like K-pop idols, put on makeup like them, and perform cover dances,' she said. 'It's a unique phenomenon not seen among other fan bases. K-pop delivers intense images that are easily imprinted, mainly through social media, and makes people want to imitate them. In the process of imitating and identifying with idols, fans are fandoming their own bodies and identities.'

Oh says that dance has become the most important component of K-pop, and that K-pop dance has already become 'a dance genre in its own right,' just as she predicted over a decade ago. She explained that K-pop dance has its own techniques developed specifically to deliver a powerful image in a single viewing through a screen rather than a theater stage, which sets it apart from other genres. As a dancer, a K-pop dance theorist, and someone who has witnessed K-pop's rise in status throughout her time in the United States, Oh expects K-pop's high popularity to continue for some time.

'K-pop Demon Hunters is enormously popular, and it has a very thick viewership among young children,' she said. 'Just as I still cannot forget Dooly, which I watched as a child, the children around the world who grow up watching K-pop Demon Hunters will become the largest future consumers of K-pop. In Korea there seems to be worry about K-pop's popularity fading, but looking at how K-pop is spreading right now, I think there is no need to worry for at least 20 years.'

Another basis for Oh's prediction of K-pop's long-term success is the growth of K-pop and K-pop dance education both inside and outside Korea. When systematic education makes reproduction possible, a path opens for the genre to sustain itself over the long term. To support this, Oh has recently completed her second book, K-pop Dance Education: To Dreamers, which is set for publication in the United States in June. The book serves as a teaching guide for K-pop dance spanning children through adult lifelong learners, and Oh expressed her hope that it will help K-pop dance and Korean culture be passed on systematically in more places around the world.
